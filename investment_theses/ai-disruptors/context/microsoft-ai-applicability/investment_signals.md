# Investment Signals Derived from AI Applicability Research

## Signal Framework for Identifying AI Disruptors

### Signal 1: Targeting High-Applicability Occupation Clusters
Companies building products for these occupation groups have the strongest tailwind:
- **Media & communications** (0.38) — content generation, editing, marketing
- **Sales & services** (0.35) — customer engagement, lead generation, CRM
- **Information & records** (0.33) — data entry, document processing, compliance
- **Mathematical/analytical** (0.32) — data science, financial modeling, research
- **Education** (0.31) — curriculum development, assessment, tutoring

### Signal 2: Addressing the Information Work Triad
The most AI-applicable work activities cluster around three functions:
1. **Creation** — writing, content generation, design
2. **Processing** — data analysis, knowledge management, synthesis
3. **Communication** — customer response, explanation, reporting

Companies that automate or augment any leg of this triad have empirical backing.

### Signal 3: Delegation vs Collaboration Product Fit
- **Delegation products** (full automation): Target activities that workers hand off entirely
  - E.g., automated customer communication, report generation, scheduling
  - Business model: Replace labor cost, charge fraction of FTE cost
  - Risk: Commoditization if the delegated task is generic

- **Collaboration products** (copilots): Target activities where professionals want AI assistance
  - E.g., coding assistants, analytical copilots, research tools
  - Business model: Productivity multiplier, charge per-seat premium
  - Risk: Must integrate deeply into existing workflows

### Signal 4: Cross-Income Applicability
AI applicability has weak wage correlation (r=0.13), meaning:
- Disruption opportunity exists across income levels, not just knowledge workers
- Companies targeting mid-wage information-adjacent roles (clerks, sales, admin) have large addressable markets
- The "AI is only for white-collar" narrative understates the opportunity

### Signal 5: Physical Work Boundary
Occupations with scores <0.10 are dominated by physical/manual work.
Companies claiming AI disruption in these spaces need a different thesis (robotics, IoT, etc.)
— pure generative AI has low applicability.

## Red Flags (Anti-Signals)
- Company claims to "replace" entire occupations — the research shows most occupations have mixed applicability
- Product targets the "connecting glue" between tasks rather than specific IWAs — this is the hardest to automate
- No clear mapping to specific work activities — vague "AI for X industry" without task-level specificity
- Targeting physical-dominant occupations with pure software AI

## Applying to Company Evaluation

### Questions to Ask About a Potential AI Disruptor:
1. **Which IWAs does their product address?** Map to the information work triad.
2. **Delegation or collaboration?** Determines business model and competitive dynamics.
3. **What occupation groups are their customers?** Higher applicability score = stronger product-market fit tailwind.
4. **Do they solve a specific task or the connecting glue?** Task-specific has more empirical support.
5. **What is the scope of their AI's impact?** Moderate+ scope correlates with sustained usage (r=0.64 with activity share).

### Quantitative Anchors from the Data
- 785 SOC codes scored — can map any company's target market to an applicability score
- Activity share threshold of 0.05% as minimum viability signal
- Completion × scope product as quality benchmark
- Full data available: github.com/microsoft/working-with-ai
