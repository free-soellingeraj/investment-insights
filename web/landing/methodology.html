From Evidence to Factor Score:
A Principled Approach to Measuring AI Adoption

How the evidence valuation pipeline transforms raw corporate disclosures into a reliable, investable AI adoption signal.

Winona Quantitative Research | February 2026

The Problem with Naive Scoring

Most attempts to quantify corporate AI adoption suffer from the same fundamental flaw: they treat all evidence as equal. A company that mentions "artificial intelligence" in its 10-K receives the same credit as one that reports "$200M in annualized cost savings from its machine learning platform." Forward-looking guidance is weighted identically to measured outcomes. The result is a noisy signal where companies skilled at AI marketing score comparably to companies skilled at AI execution. This problem mirrors a well-understood challenge in factor investing: the raw characteristic must be carefully transformed before it can serve as a reliable scoring signal. Our evidence valuation pipeline addresses this by introducing a structured intermediary stage between evidence extraction and index scoring — one that classifies, values, and contextualizes each piece of evidence before it contributes to a company's AI Opportunity Factor score.

Evidence Classification: Plan, Investment, Capture

Drawing on the distinction between "applied" and "mentioned" AI adoption identified in recent computational linguistics research (Frontiers in Artificial Intelligence, 2025), we classify every extracted evidence passage into one of three types. Plan evidence captures forward-looking statements and strategic guidance — "we intend to deploy generative AI across customer service by 2027." Investment evidence reflects committed action — "we deployed a computer vision system across three manufacturing divisions last quarter, with $45M in associated capital expenditure." Capture evidence documents measured outcomes — "AI-driven automation reduced customer support costs by 33%, saving approximately $120M annually."

This taxonomy is not merely a label; it determines both the structure of the valuation (plans require probability estimates and time horizons, captures require before/after metrics) and the weight assigned in the final factor score. The classification draws on the methodology of Ante and Saggu (2025), whose "Quantifying a Firm's AI Engagement" demonstrated that multiplicative scoring of AI mentions in 10-K filings — weighting by specificity and context — produces signals validated against subsequent stock returns.

Factor Score Formula

group_score = specificity × magnitude × stage_weight × recency
specificity — 0–1: concreteness vs. boilerplate
magnitude — 0–1: dollar estimate / revenue
stage_weight — plan=0.3, investment=0.7, capture=1.0
recency — 0.7years_old, floor 0.3

The Valuation Pipeline: From Passages to Dollar Estimates

Each evidence group passes through a two-stage valuation process. In the preliminary stage, an LLM independently evaluates the group's passages, classifies the evidence type, estimates a dollar range for the potential or realized impact, and produces a structured narrative. Plan evidence receives probability-weighted horizon estimates; investment evidence receives deployment scope and completion assessments; capture evidence receives measured metric deltas and annualized dollar impacts.

In the regressive final stage, the LLM re-evaluates each group in the context of all prior final valuations for the same company. This sequential processing — conceptually analogous to the Fama-French practice of controlling for one characteristic while measuring another — allows the system to identify and flag discrepancies between evidence groups. When a company's 10-K claims "$500M in AI savings" but its earnings call reports only "$50M in identified efficiencies," the newer, more specific evidence is trusted, and the discrepancy is recorded with an explicit resolution rationale.

The Factor Score: Multiplicative Construction

The final factor score for each evidence group is computed as the product of four components: specificity (how concrete and detailed the evidence is, versus boilerplate), magnitude (the dollar estimate scaled relative to company revenue), stage weight (plan = 0.3, investment = 0.7, capture = 1.0), and recency (exponential time decay with a floor). This multiplicative construction, adapted from Ante and Saggu's validated approach, ensures that a single highly specific, large-magnitude, recently captured outcome contributes more to a company's score than a dozen vague, undated plans.

The approach also draws a conceptual parallel to factor index construction methodology: just as Fama and French (1993) established that turning a continuous firm-level characteristic into a reliable factor requires careful normalization, breakpoint determination, and cross-sectional ranking, our pipeline transforms raw LLM-extracted evidence into a normalized, comparable score through structured valuation and multiplicative weighting. The company-level aggregation separates potential value (dollar estimates from plan and investment evidence) from actual value (dollar estimates from capture evidence only), giving investors two distinct lenses on AI adoption — what a company says it will do, and what it has demonstrably done.

From Score to Index: Portfolio-Ready Output

The evidence valuation pipeline produces scores that feed directly into the AI Opportunity Index's factor construction. Per-dimension scores (cost, revenue, general) are aggregated from final valuations, with diminishing returns at the upper bound to prevent any single company from being dominated by evidence volume rather than evidence quality. These scores integrate with the existing 4-value framework — Cost Opportunity, Cost Capture, Revenue Opportunity, Revenue Capture — where the valuation pipeline replaces the previous naive confidence-summing approach for capture scoring.

The result is a factor that can be applied in the style of modern index providers: companies are ranked by their AI Adoption Factor score, weights are tilted proportionally (score-tilted market capitalization weighting per industry standard), and the index is reconstituted as new evidence is processed. Unlike thematic AI indices that make a binary inclusion decision ("is this an AI company?"), the factor approach scores every company on a continuous scale — capturing not just which companies are in the AI business, but how deeply any company is adopting AI across its operations, and crucially, whether that adoption is producing measurable economic value.
