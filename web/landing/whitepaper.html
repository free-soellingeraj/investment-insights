The AI Opportunity Index:
A 4-Value Scoring Framework

A systematic methodology for evaluating public companies' AI potential and execution across cost and revenue dimensions.

Winona Quantitative Research | February 2026

Abstract

The AI Opportunity Index quantifies how much artificial intelligence could impact a public company and how effectively that company is capturing that opportunity. Unlike consensus AI rankings that rely on a single composite score, our framework decomposes AI exposure into four independent values: Cost Opportunity, Cost Capture, Revenue Opportunity, and Revenue Capture. Each is derived from distinct public data sources and scored independently, enabling nuanced comparisons that distinguish between structural potential and demonstrated execution. A fifth tracked value, General AI Investment, captures broad AI commitments that are not specifically targeted at cost or revenue outcomes, preventing unverifiable announcements from inflating capture scores.

1. Motivation

Most AI investment frameworks suffer from two fundamental problems. First, they blend potential and execution into a single number, making it impossible to distinguish a company with enormous untapped AI potential from one that has already captured it. Second, they mix cost-side and revenue-side effects, obscuring whether a company benefits from AI through internal efficiency or through selling AI-powered products.

The AI Opportunity Index addresses both problems by scoring every company on a 2x2 matrix: Cost vs. Revenue on one axis, Opportunity (structural potential) vs. Capture (demonstrated execution) on the other. This produces four scores per company, plus derived ROI metrics that reveal whether a company is over-investing, under-investing, or efficiently converting its AI opportunity into realized value.

The framework is designed around a principle we call honest classification: when a company announces a "$2B AI investment" without specifying whether it targets cost reduction or revenue growth, we classify that as General AI Investment rather than artificially splitting it across dimensions. This prevents vague announcements from inflating capture scores and keeps the four core values clean and verifiable.

2. Framework Overview

Opportunity Dimension

Measures structural AI potential based on industry composition, workforce characteristics, and customer base.

  • Cost Opportunity: How automatable is this company's workforce? Derived from occupation-level AI applicability scores mapped through industry-occupation matrices.
  • Revenue Opportunity: Can this company sell AI to its customers? Derived from customer industry AI applicability and B2B positioning.

Capture Dimension

Measures demonstrated AI execution from four independent signal sources, each classified by target and stage.

  • Cost Capture: Aggregated from cost-targeted evidence across all sub-scorers (automation patents, MLOps hiring, efficiency-related filing language).
  • Revenue Capture: Aggregated from revenue-targeted evidence (AI product launches, product-facing patents, AI product manager hiring).

General AI Investment (5th Value)

Broad AI investment announcements that do not specify cost or revenue focus. Tracked as a separate metric to provide a measure of total AI commitment, but explicitly excluded from Cost Capture and Revenue Capture calculations. This prevents companies from inflating their capture scores with unverifiable general announcements.

Derived Metrics

From the four core values, we derive ROI metrics that measure capital efficiency:

  • Cost ROI = Cost Capture / Cost Opportunity (capped to prevent extreme values when opportunity is near zero)
  • Revenue ROI = Revenue Capture / Revenue Opportunity (similarly capped)
  • Combined ROI = Weighted average of Cost ROI and Revenue ROI

Companies with ROI > 1.0 are capturing more than their structural opportunity suggests, indicating aggressive AI execution. Companies with ROI near 0 have significant untapped potential.

3. Opportunity Scoring

The opportunity dimension estimates structural AI potential independent of any company actions. It answers: "Given this company's industry, workforce, and customer base, how much value could AI create?"

3.1 Data Sources

  • BLS Occupation-Industry Matrix: Maps industries to their workforce composition by Standard Occupation Classification (SOC) groups.
  • Microsoft AI Applicability Scores: Per-occupation AI applicability ratings from academic research, providing a granular estimate of how much each job function can be augmented or automated by AI.
  • SIC/NAICS Industry Codes: Map each company to its industry classification, enabling workforce composition estimation.
  • Yahoo Finance Sector/Industry: Fallback classification when SIC codes are unavailable, with sector-specific AI profiles.

3.2 Cost Opportunity

Cost Opportunity measures how much AI could reduce a company's internal operating costs. The score is derived by mapping the company's industry to its typical occupation mix, then computing the weighted average AI applicability of those occupations. Employee count provides a scaling factor: larger workforces represent more absolute savings potential.

The raw applicability average is normalized to a 0-1 scale with bounds calibrated to the empirical distribution of scores across all industries.

3.3 Revenue Opportunity

Revenue Opportunity measures how much AI could grow a company's top line. For B2B companies, this is primarily about selling AI solutions to customers. The score is derived from the AI applicability of customer industries (e.g., a software company selling to financial services evaluates how much financial services workers could benefit from AI). B2B technology and services companies receive a positioning boost, reflecting their structural advantage in packaging AI capabilities as products.

For non-B2B companies, revenue opportunity reflects the potential to embed AI in consumer-facing products and services, scored more conservatively.

4. Capture Scoring

The capture dimension measures what a company is actually doing with AI. It aggregates evidence from four independent sub-scorers, each contributing a weighted share to the final capture scores.

4.1 Evidence Classification

Every piece of evidence produced by any sub-scorer is classified on two independent axes before aggregation:

Target Dimension

  • Cost: Evidence of AI used to reduce internal costs (automation, efficiency, process optimization)
  • Revenue: Evidence of AI used to generate revenue (AI products, AI-powered services, customer-facing features)
  • General: AI investment without clear cost/revenue specificity (tracked separately)

Capture Stage

  • Planned: Announced intentions, roadmap items, strategy statements
  • Invested: Actual spending, hiring, patent filing, product development
  • Realized: Announced results with quantifiable metrics (cost savings, revenue figures)

4.2 Sub-Scorer A: SEC Filing NLP

Analyzes the text of a company's most recent SEC filing (10-K or 10-Q) for AI-related language. The scorer uses a tiered keyword system that weights specific AI terminology (e.g., "large language model," "neural network") more heavily than generic terms (e.g., "automation," "digital transformation"). Each keyword match is classified by examining the surrounding text for cost or revenue context.

When available, an LLM-based scorer supplements the keyword approach. The LLM evaluates strategic importance, specificity, and investment evidence, and provides independent cost/revenue/general capture assessments with stage classifications. The final score blends LLM and keyword results.

Stage classification uses contextual cues: "plan to deploy" maps to Planned, "implemented across divisions" maps to Invested, "reduced costs by $X million" maps to Realized.

4.3 Sub-Scorer B: AI Product Analysis

Scans recent news and press releases for AI product signals. Distinct signal types include: AI product launches (scored by specificity), shipped products with user/revenue attribution, and AI partnerships with major AI providers. Each article is classified based on content analysis:

  • External product launches are classified as Revenue/Invested
  • Shipped products with revenue attribution are Revenue/Realized
  • Internal tool deployments are Cost/Invested
  • Cost savings announcements are Cost/Realized
  • Broad AI strategy announcements are General/Planned

4.4 Sub-Scorer C: AI Job Signals

Estimates the ratio of AI/ML job postings relative to total hiring. When job aggregator API data is available, the ratio is computed directly. Otherwise, a heuristic estimate is applied based on company characteristics and known AI intensity of peer companies.

Job titles are classified by target dimension when individual title data is available: product-focused AI roles (e.g., "AI Product Manager," "AI Solutions Architect") map to Revenue, while infrastructure roles (e.g., "MLOps Engineer," "Automation Engineer") map to Cost. Ambiguous AI/ML roles default to General rather than an artificial 50/50 split. All job evidence is classified as Invested stage.

4.5 Sub-Scorer D: Patent Signals

Queries the USPTO PatentsView API for AI-related patents filed by the company. Patent scoring uses a tiered system based on total AI patent count and applies a recency bonus for recent filings. Classification uses CPC codes and abstract text analysis:

  • Revenue-oriented CPC codes (computer vision, speech processing, NLP) map to Revenue
  • Cost-oriented abstract keywords (optimization, scheduling, automation, anomaly detection) map to Cost
  • Patents without clear cost/revenue signal default to General

All patent evidence is classified as Invested stage, reflecting active R&D commitment.

5. Capture Aggregation

The four sub-scorers are combined through weighted aggregation, with each sub-scorer contributing a configurable share of the total. The aggregation maintains strict dimensional separation:

  • Cost Capture = weighted average of each sub-scorer's cost-targeted evidence scores
  • Revenue Capture = weighted average of each sub-scorer's revenue-targeted evidence scores
  • General AI Investment = weighted average of each sub-scorer's general-targeted evidence scores

When a sub-scorer returns no evidence (e.g., no patents found), it is excluded from the weighted average rather than contributing a zero, preventing data availability gaps from penalizing companies.

5.1 Discrepancy Flags

The system generates automated discrepancy flags when opportunity and capture diverge significantly. These flags help identify companies worth investigating further:

  • "Significant AI cost savings potential unrealized" — high Cost Opportunity, low Cost Capture
  • "AI revenue overweighted vs industry baseline" — Revenue Capture exceeds Revenue Opportunity
  • "AI investment not translating to targeted outcomes" — high General Investment but low Cost + Revenue Capture
  • "High AI discussion in filings but low product evidence" — possible AI-washing
  • "Heavy AI hiring but few visible AI products" — likely in build phase

6. Index Construction

The four core values are used to compute composite scores for quadrant classification. A composite opportunity score and composite capture score are each a weighted average of their cost and revenue components. Companies are then assigned to quadrants based on whether their composite scores exceed configurable thresholds:

Untapped Potential

High Opportunity
Low Capture

AI Leaders

High Opportunity
High Capture

AI-Resistant

Low Opportunity

For portfolio construction, companies are ranked by composite score and selected into index variants with configurable weighting schemes (equal-weighted and score-weighted). The index is reconstituted periodically as scores are refreshed with new data.

8. Limitations and Considerations

  • Public data only: The framework relies entirely on publicly available data. Companies with significant AI activity that is not reflected in filings, patents, job postings, or press releases will be underscored.
  • Industry classification granularity: SIC-to-NAICS mapping and occupation matrices operate at industry group levels. Individual company workforce composition may differ significantly from industry averages.
  • Keyword and pattern limitations: NLP scoring relies on keyword matching and context window analysis. Sophisticated AI strategies described in non-standard language may be missed.
  • General classification conservatism: The honest classification principle means that ambiguous AI evidence defaults to General rather than being split across Cost and Revenue. This produces conservative capture scores but avoids artificial precision.
  • Temporal lag: SEC filings reflect past reporting periods. Patent data has publication delays. The capture dimension inherently lags real-time company activity.
  • No proprietary data: Unlike strategies that use satellite imagery, credit card data, or web scraping of private platforms, this index uses only structured public APIs and government datasets.

9. Conclusion

The AI Opportunity Index provides a structured, transparent framework for evaluating companies' AI positioning. By separating opportunity from capture and cost from revenue, investors can identify distinct categories of AI exposure: companies with massive untapped potential, companies efficiently converting opportunity into execution, and companies whose AI investments may be misallocated relative to their structural opportunity.

The framework's honest classification of evidence, configurable weighting system, and multi-source scoring ensemble are designed to produce scores that are defensible, interpretable, and actionable. All scoring parameters are centrally configured and documented, enabling systematic sensitivity analysis and methodology evolution as the AI landscape develops.
